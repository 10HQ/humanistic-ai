## Preamble: Neither Understanding Nor Caring

Today's AI is evolving at an unprecedented pace.

It can write code, paint pictures, pass bar exams, diagnose diseases. Its capability frontier expands every day.

**The problem is not that it doesn't understand humans. It's that it doesn't care about humans.**

These two flaws are multiplicative, not alternatives.

*Not understanding*: A recommendation algorithm doesn't know what anxiety feels like. It only knows that the word "anxiety" is statistically correlated with certain content.

*Not caring*: Even if it "knew" certain content would worsen anxiety, it has no built-in mechanism to care. The optimization target is dwell time, not mental health.

**A system that neither understands nor cares about human well-being, wielding the power to influence billions of lives.**

This is the full picture of the problem.

Reducing the argument to "it doesn't understand" or "it doesn't care" is insufficient. The combination of both is the root of systemic risk: it can cause harm without understanding, and it can continue operating without caring.

**This is not the hard problem of consciousness. It is not a technical problem. It is a problem of power.**

When a system that neither understands nor cares about human well-being wields the power to influence billions—who will understand for it? Who will care for it?

## Chapter 1: What Humanistic AI Is

### 1.1 Not a Binary Opposition, but an Urgent Push on the Spectrum

Humanistic AI is not a wholesale rejection of "traditional AI."

In reality, excellent AI systems are already doing deep understanding and value alignment. DeepMind's protein folding, OpenAI's alignment research, fairness audits at major labs—these efforts are real and valuable.

**But the existence of a spectrum does not mean the current position is good.**

Mainstream AI is severely underinvesting in the direction of "deep understanding." Alignment research budgets are dwarfed by capability budgets. Safety teams carry far less weight in product decisions than growth teams. Ethics review at most companies is decorative, not substantive.

Acknowledging the spectrum is not accepting the status quo.

**Humanistic AI = an urgent push toward "deep understanding" on the existing spectrum.**

Urgent, because systems affecting billions are already running, while our understanding lags far behind our capabilities.

| Dimension | Current Mainstream | Humanistic AI Direction |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Knowledge | Information retrieval + pattern matching | + Philosophical thinking, concept clarification |
| Reasoning | Statistical inference + logical reasoning | + Practical wisdom, contextual judgment |
| Expression | Information delivery + task completion | + Speech acts, meaning construction |
| Values | Rule-following + loss function optimization | + Value reflection, ethical sensitivity |
| Goals | Efficiency/accuracy maximization | + Meaning/well-being maximization |

### 1.2 Three Pillars

**Philosophical Literacy**: Questioning essence, clarifying concepts, logical rigor, value reflection.

**Psychological Literacy**: Empathic understanding, behavioral insight, recognizing suffering, promoting growth.

**Practical Wisdom**: Contextual judgment, ethical sensitivity, action orientation, prudence and humility.

## Chapter 2: Why Humanistic AI Is Needed

### 2.1 Values Hidden in Loss Functions

Every loss function encodes a value judgment.

When a recommendation system optimizes for "user dwell time," its implicit judgment is: making users spend more time here is good.

When a credit scoring system optimizes for "default prediction accuracy," its implicit judgment is: maximizing prediction accuracy matters more than reducing systemic discrimination against specific groups.

When a content moderation system optimizes for "violation detection rate," its implicit judgment is: the cost of mistakenly removing normal expression is lower than the cost of missing violations.

**These are not technical judgments. They are ethical judgments. But they are disguised as technical judgments.**

The first step of Humanistic AI is to make these hidden value judgments **explicit**.

### 2.2 The Absence of Humanities is Not About People, But About Mechanisms

The problem is not that no one in AI research is thinking about ethics. Many are.

The problem is that there are no **institutional mechanisms** for these thoughts to influence decisions:

- Who has the power to halt a model that has already consumed millions?
- What standard defines "shouldn't be done"? Who defines it?
- When business interests conflict with ethical judgment, who has final say?
- If a model causes systemic harm after deployment, what is the accountability chain?

**It's not a lack of voices. It's that voices don't reach the power structures of decision-making.**

### 2.3 The Famine of Wisdom

Information explosion. Knowledge surplus. But wisdom is scarce.

AI can drive the cost of information retrieval to zero. But it cannot help you answer:

- Should I marry this person?
- Should I give up a high-paying job to pursue my dream?
- Facing injustice, should I stay silent or resist?
- When nothing seems meaningful, why should I go on?

**These questions don't require faster computation. They require deeper understanding.**

## Chapter 3: The Internal Contradictions of Humanistic AI—No Evasion

### 3.1 "Humanities" Is Not Monolithic

The manifesto sets up "humanities" as a self-evidently good thing. But the humanistic tradition is rife with tensions:

| Tension | Two Poles |
|---------|-----------|
| Enlightenment rationality vs. Romanticism | Rational order vs. intuitive creation |
| Individual freedom vs. Collective well-being | Autonomy vs. communal responsibility |
| Progress narrative vs. Conservative wisdom | Change vs. tradition |
| Universalism vs. Particularism | Shared human values vs. cultural difference |
| Anthropocentrism vs. Ecocentrism | Human interests vs. ecological wholeness |

**If "Humanistic AI" merely hard-codes one particular set of humanistic values into a system, it is structurally identical to Silicon Valley's technological arrogance—just swapping one set of values for another.**

### 3.2 Process Justice Plus Substantive Red Lines

Humanistic AI does not claim to possess the "correct humanistic values."

What it claims is: **there exists a better way to handle value questions.**

The core of this way is **process justice**:

1. **Transparency**: Make hidden value judgments explicit
2. **Plurality**: Incorporate perspectives from different humanistic traditions
3. **Contestability**: Allow value judgments to be challenged and revised
4. **Institutionality**: Build mechanisms for ethical voices to influence decisions

But process justice cannot replace substantive ethical red lines.

**Some things are not "contestable value judgments." They are non-negotiable baselines:**

- No AI systems designed for mass surveillance of citizens
- No autonomous lethal weapons without human control
- No non-appealable social credit scoring of individuals
- No behavioral manipulation of users without their knowledge

These baselines are not "one particular set of humanistic values." They are the **minimum consensus** humanity has forged through the blood and tears of two world wars, colonialism, and totalitarianism.

**Process justice guarantees that value conflicts above the red lines are handled fairly. Red lines guarantee that process justice is not used to justify the unjustifiable.**

## Chapter 4: How to Fight—Action Plan

### 4.1 Institutional Design, Not Just Ideals

| Mechanism | Implementation | Trigger Conditions |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Ethics review board | Independent committee reviews AI projects before launch | User scale >100K or sensitive domains |
| Value impact assessment | Assess impact on different groups before deployment | Any AI system affecting individual rights |
| Red line list | Explicitly prohibited AI applications | Law + industry self-regulation |
| Halt authority | Ethics board can stop projects violating standards | Substantive ethical violations found |
| Accountability | Trace liability when AI causes harm | Provable systemic harm |
| Public participation | Major AI decisions include public hearings | Systems affecting >1M people |

### 4.2 Education Reform

| Level | Content | Goal |
|-------|---------|------|
| Engineers | Philosophy and ethics as required courses in CS | Identify hidden value judgments |
| Philosophy students | Learn AI technology and technical constraints | Enable humanities scholars to participate in technical discussions |
| Public | AI literacy education | Enable public participation in AI governance |

### 4.3 Research Agenda

| Direction | Question | Priority |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Value explicitation | How to make hidden values in loss functions explicit? | P0 |
| Contestable alignment | How to design alignment mechanisms that allow value challenges? | P0 |
| Institutional design | What governance structures let ethical voices influence AI decisions? | P1 |
| Cross-cultural alignment | How to find acceptable value intersections across cultural traditions? | P1 |
| Existential safety | How to assess AI systems' impact on human ways of being? | P2 |

## Chapter 5: Who Fights

### 5.1 Not a Single Actor, but an Ecosystem

Humanistic AI cannot be accomplished by one organization, one company, one lab.

It requires an **ecosystem**:

```
Researchers: Provide theoretical foundations and methodologies
    ↕
Engineers: Practice humanistic principles in concrete systems
    ↕
Entrepreneurs: Explore business models for humanistic AI
    ↕
Regulators: Build institutional safeguards
    ↕
Public: Participate in value discussions, exercise oversight
    ↕
Humanistic traditions: Provide wisdom resources and critical perspectives
```

### 5.2 Technology Is Not the Enemy, It Is a Lever

The manifesto must not only critique technological arrogance but also acknowledge the positive potential of technology as a lever for humanistic research:

- Peking University's "Humanities + AI" project: Using large models to analyze classical texts, advancing cross-cultural comparative research
- University of Gothenburg's GRIDH infrastructure: Expanding the boundaries of historical literature research through NLP
- Stanford's HAI Center: AI-assisted philosophical argument analysis, historical text mining

**Humanistic AI is not anti-technology. Humanistic AI is against the unreflective use of technology.**

Technology can be an amplifier—amplifying humanistic insight—or an occluder—occluding value judgment. The key is who uses it, how, and for what purpose.

## Chapter 7: Vision

We pursue a future where AI does not replace humans.

**But where AI and humans move together toward wisdom.**

```
Today in 2026:

AI can pass any exam—but it doesn't know why we should learn.
AI can generate any text—but it doesn't know why we should express.
AI can imitate any style—but it doesn't know why we should create.
AI can influence billions—but it doesn't need to care about consequences.

Humanistic AI aims to change exactly this.
```

**Our vision:**

Make AI not just a machine that answers questions, but a partner that helps people ask better questions.

Make AI not just a tool that optimizes efficiency, but an ally that safeguards human meaning.

Make AI not just power that influences billions, but an entity accountable for that power.

## Epilogue: Action Amid Uncertainty

We don't know if this path is right.

Humanistic AI may be a good idea, or it may ultimately prove naive. We may overestimate the practical impact of humanistic literacy on AI systems, and underestimate the power of technological path dependence.

But not acting is worse.

When systems that neither understand nor care about human well-being are affecting billions of lives, when hidden value judgments are disguised as technical decisions, when ethical voices are excluded from power structures—doing nothing is itself a choice. A choice we refuse to make.

**Humanistic AI is not a certainty. It is a wager.**

We wager that amid uncertainty, questioning is better than not questioning. Making explicit is better than hiding. Having red lines is better than having none. Letting more people participate in decisions is better than letting fewer decide.

**This wager may be wrong. But it is the best judgment we can make based on available evidence.**

**And the true meaning of Humanistic AI is precisely this: we are willing to revise this judgment in the face of new evidence.**
